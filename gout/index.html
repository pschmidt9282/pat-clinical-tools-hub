Gout — Flare Management & Chronic ULT

Interactive decision-making matrix for gout. Differentiate acute flare agents (Colchicine, NSAIDs, Prednisone) and chronic urate-lowering therapy (Allopurinol, Febuxostat, Probenecid) by kidney disease (CKD), cardiovascular risks, and HLA-B*5801 testing.

HLA-B*5801 Testing Guideline: The ACR strongly recommends testing for the HLA-B*5801 allele prior to starting Allopurinol in patients of Han Chinese, Thai, Korean (with CKD ≥3), or African American descent. Positive results indicate a high risk of fatal Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS).

Colchicine Acute Flare & Prophylaxis Avoid if CrCl <10; Dose adjust in CKD Safe (Anti-inflammatory benefit) No Screen Required Severe Diarrhea, Nausea, Cramps 1.2 mg at flare onset, then 0.6 mg 1 hour later (Max 1.8 mg total). Prophylaxis: 0.6 mg daily/BID. **Contraindicated with strong CYP3A4 / P-gp inhibitors in CKD** (can cause fatal neuromyopathy). First-line for acute flares (within 36h of onset) and chronic ULT initiation prophylaxis. $$ (Moderate Generic)
NSAIDs Naproxen, Indomethacin, Celecoxib (Acute) Avoid in eGFR <30 (AKI & hyperkalemia risk) Avoid in severe HF / post-MI (fluid retention) No Screen Required GI bleeding, dyspepsia, ulcer risk Naproxen 500 mg BID, Indomethacin 50 mg TID, or Celecoxib 200 mg BID. Taper as flare resolves. Active peptic ulcer, severe CKD, decompensated HF, anticoagulation. First-line for acute flares in young patients without renal or cardiovascular comorbidities. $ (Very Low Cost)
Corticosteroids Prednisone (Oral), Triamcinolone (IA) Preferred flare agent in CKD Caution (Fluid retention/BP elevation) No Screen Required Hyperglycemia, mood changes, insomnia Oral Prednisone 30–40 mg daily for 2–5 days, then taper over 7–10 days. Intra-articular if monoarticular. Systemic fungal infections, uncontrolled diabetes (severe hyperglycemia). First-line for flares, particularly when CKD or NSAID/colchicine contraindications exist. $ (Very Low Cost)
Allopurinol Chronic ULT (Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor) Start low in CKD (≤50mg); titrate to sUA target Safe (First-line choice) **High Risk** (Screen high-risk ancestry) Pruritus, rash, cytopenias Start at 100 mg daily (≤50 mg in CKD). Titrate up by 50–100 mg every 2–5 weeks until sUA < 6.0 mg/dL. Concomitant Azathioprine or Mercaptopurine (causes fatal cytopenias; avoid combination). **First-line choice for chronic ULT** in all patients (including CKD). $ (Very Low Cost)
Febuxostat Chronic ULT (Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor) No dose adjustment in mild-mod CKD **Boxed Warning** (CVD mortality risk) Safe (No HLA association) Hepatic enzyme elevation, arthralgia, rash Start at 40 mg daily. Increase to 80 mg daily if sUA target (<6.0) is not met in 2 weeks. Concomitant Azathioprine/Mercaptopurine. Prior history of cardiovascular events (relative). Second-line chronic ULT, restricted to patients intolerant or unresponsive to allopurinol. $$ (Moderate Generic)
Probenecid Chronic ULT (Uricosuric) **Ineffective if eGFR <30**; raises stone risk Safe No Screen Required GI distress, uric acid renal stones (nephrolithiasis) Start 250 mg BID. Increase to 500 mg BID, up to 2 g/day. Maintain high fluid intake. Concomitant Salicylates (aspirin blocks probenecid efficacy). History of renal stones. Alternative chronic ULT if xanthine oxidase inhibitors are contraindicated or ineffective. $$ (Moderate Generic)
