Section 1: Screening, Diagnosis & Treatment Thresholds

USPSTF Screening Guidelines

  • All postmenopausal women ≥65 years
  • Postmenopausal women <65 years with increased risk of osteoporosis (determined by a formal clinical risk assessment tool like FRAX or Osteoporosis Self-Assessment Tool).

Diagnostic Criteria

Osteoporosis is diagnosed by either:

  1. T-score ≤ −2.5 at the femoral neck, total hip, lumbar spine, or 33% radius on DXA.
  2. Clinical Diagnosis: A low-trauma hip or vertebral fracture, regardless of T-score.

US Treatment Thresholds (BHOF/Endocrine Society)

Pharmacotherapy is indicated for patients meeting any of the following:

  • T-score ≤ −2.5 at femoral neck, total hip, lumbar spine, or 33% radius
  • Low-trauma hip or vertebral fracture (clinical osteoporosis)
  • Osteopenia (T-score between −1.0 and −2.5) PLUS a FRAX 10-year probability of:
    • Major osteoporotic fracture (MOF) ≥20% OR
    • Hip fracture ≥3%

ASBMR/BHOF Goal-Directed Treatment Framework

Select initial therapy based on the probability of achieving a target T-score > −2.5 at the total hip within 3 years. This requires stratifying risk levels immediately at baseline.

Baseline Risk Stratification for Initial Therapy Selection

High Fracture Risk
  • T-score ≤ −2.5 at qualifying site WITHOUT recent fracture
  • Osteopenia meeting FRAX thresholds (≥20% MOF / ≥3% hip)
Preferred Initial Agents: Oral/IV Bisphosphonates or Denosumab
Very High Fracture Risk
  • T-score ≤ −2.5 PLUS recent fracture (within 12 mo)
  • Multiple vertebral fractures
  • Very low T-score (e.g., T-score ≤ −3.0)
  • High fall risk or FRAX MOF >30% / Hip >4.5%
Preferred Initial Agents: Anabolic-First Strategy (Romosozumab, Teriparatide, Abaloparatide) followed by antiresorptive

Section 1B: Calcium & Vitamin D Supplementation Complete Reference

All major guidelines recommend calcium and vitamin D as mandatory adjuncts to all osteoporosis pharmacotherapy, since virtually every pivotal drug trial included them in both treatment and placebo arms. Several agents carry specific risks of hypocalcemia that make adequate supplementation a safety requirement.

Vitamin D Deficiency Repletion Protocol (AACE 2020)

If 25(OH)D < 20 ng/mL:

  • Repletion: Ergocalciferol (D2) 50,000 IU orally once weekly for 8 weeks (or daily D3 5,000–6,000 IU/day).
  • Maintenance: D3 1,000–2,000 IU daily once levels are stable.
  • Important: Avoid intermittent high-dose boluses (e.g. 50,000–100,000 IU monthly) as boluses paradoxically increase fall/fracture risks.

Agent-Specific Calcium & Vitamin D Requirements

Abaloparatide Standard supplementation. Monitor for hypercalcemia. Same as Teriparatide.

Raloxifene Standard supplementation. Baseline 25(OH)D.

🔑 Key Clinical Pearls for Calcium

  • PPI / Gastric Acid Blockers: PPIs and H2 blockers reduce gastric acid, significantly impairing calcium carbonate absorption. Always switch to Calcium Citrate in these patients.
  • GI Side Effects: Calcium carbonate causes more constipation, bloating, and gas than calcium citrate.
  • Nephrolithiasis Risk: WHI trials showed a 17% increase in renal stones with 1,000 mg calcium supplements on top of ~1,100 mg diet. Keep total intake ≤1,200 mg/day.
  • Cardiovascular Risk: Meta-analyses suggest calcium supplements (not dietary calcium) may increase CV events, though controversial. Emphasize dietary sources first.

🌲 Quick-Reference Formulation Decision Tree

  • Most patients: Calcium carbonate 500–600 mg with meals, 1–2 times daily.
  • On PPI, H2RA, or achlorhydria: Calcium citrate 315 mg, 2–3 times daily (with/without food).
  • Post-bariatric surgery (bypass, sleeve): Calcium citrate (carbonate is poorly absorbed).
  • Constipation from carbonate: Switch to calcium citrate.
  • Dietary intake already ≥1,000–1,200 mg/day: No supplement needed; avoid excess.

Section 2: Oral Bisphosphonates (First-Line Antiresorptives)

Oral bisphosphonates are the standard first-line therapies for the prevention and treatment of osteoporosis in patients at high risk. They bind to bone hydroxyapatite and inhibit osteoclast-mediated resorption.

⚠️ Critical Administration Instructions (Mandatory for Efficacy & GI Protection)

  1. Take first thing in the morning immediately after waking up and before any food or other drinks.
  2. Swallow the tablet whole with 6–8 oz of plain water only (mineral water, sparkling water, coffee, tea, and juice are contraindicated).
  3. Wait at least 30 minutes (60 minutes for ibandronate) before consuming your first food, drink (other than plain water), or taking other medications.
  4. Remain completely upright (sitting, standing, or walking) for at least 30 minutes. Do not lie down until after you have eaten food.
  5. Risedronate DR (Delayed-Release) Exception: Take 35 mg immediately after breakfast with at least 4 oz of plain water. Patient must still remain upright for 30 minutes.
Clinical Note on Ibandronate: Ibandronate has only demonstrated efficacy in reducing vertebral fractures. It has no proven benefit for hip or nonvertebral fractures in large RCTs. Because of this, it is not recommended as a preferred first-line agent in major clinical guidelines.

GI Contraindications

  • Active esophageal disorders (achalasia, strictures, Barrett's esophagus)
  • Inability to sit or stand upright for at least 30 minutes

Compliance & Preferences

  • Documented history of non-adherence or intolerance to oral therapy
  • Patient preference for infrequent dosing (yearly vs. weekly/monthly)

Section 4: RANKL Inhibitor (Denosumab)

Denosumab (Prolia) is a fully human monoclonal antibody that binds and neutralizes RANKL, preventing osteoclast formation and survival. It is highly effective but requires a strict administration schedule due to rebound fracture risks.

4A. Denosumab Dosing and Administration

Dose60 mg subcutaneously every 6 months
Dosing Interval WindowStrictly within 6 months (delaying >1 month triggers rebound risk)
Dosing SitesUpper arm, upper thigh, or abdomen
AdministrationBy healthcare provider in clinical setting
Pre-TreatmentCorrect hypocalcemia; ensure Vit D sufficiency; check eGFR
Renal AdjustmentNo dose adjustment needed (not cleared renally)
FDA IndicationsPMO (high risk); male OP; GIOP; ADT-related (prostate cancer); AI-related (breast cancer)
Cost~$1,500 / 6-month dose (brand); biosimilars available

🖤 FDA BLACK BOX WARNING: Severe Hypocalcemia in Advanced CKD

Patients with advanced chronic kidney disease (eGFR <30 mL/min or on dialysis) are at a significantly increased risk of severe, life-threatening hypocalcemia.

Mandatory Strategy: If used in CKD stage 4-5, consult a nephrologist or CKD-MBD specialist, ensure aggressive calcium and vitamin D supplementation, and monitor serum calcium closely (e.g., 10-14 days post-injection).

🚨 Critical Warning: Rebound Vertebral Fractures & Discontinuation Plan

The Rebound Phenomener: Unlike bisphosphonates, denosumab does not accumulate in the bone matrix. Once denosumab is stopped or delayed by >1 month, bone resorption rebounds rapidly, causing bone mineral density (BMD) to drop back to baseline within 12 months and triggering an 8–10% risk of multiple vertebral fractures.

Mandatory Transition Protocol

Patients who stop denosumab must immediately transition to an alternative antiresorptive (bisphosphonate):

1
Preferred Option: Zoledronic Acid (IV)
Administer 5 mg IV zoledronic acid 6 to 7 months after the final denosumab dose.
2
Monitoring Transition:
Measure serum CTX (bone turnover marker) at 3-month intervals. If bone resorption rebounds (CTX rises), a second zoledronic acid infusion may be needed 3–6 months later.
3
Alternative Option: Alendronate (Oral)
Alendronate 70 mg weekly starting 6 months after the last denosumab dose (though weekly oral therapy may not prevent BMD loss as reliably as IV zoledronic acid).
❌ Contraindicated Transition: Do NOT switch directly from denosumab to teriparatide or abaloparatide. This sequence causes rapid and severe BMD loss, particularly at the hip and cortical bone.

Section 5: Anabolic Agents (Bone Builders)

Anabolic agents stimulate bone formation and are recommended as first-line therapy for patients at very high fracture risk. Beginning with an anabolic agent followed by an antiresorptive (anabolic-first sequence) yields much larger long-term BMD gains than the reverse.

Contraindications: PTH Receptor Agonists (Teriparatide & Abaloparatide)

  • Bone metastases or history of skeletal malignancies
  • Paget's disease of bone
  • Prior external beam or implant skeletal radiation
  • Unexplained elevated serum alkaline phosphatase
  • Pre-existing hypercalcemia or primary hyperparathyroidism
  • Open epiphyses (pediatric/young adult)

Contraindications: Romosozumab (Evenity)

  • Myocardial infarction (MI) or stroke within the preceding year (FDA Black Box)
  • Pre-existing untreated hypocalcemia
  • Known hypersensitivity
First-Dose Orthostatic Precautions: Both teriparatide and abaloparatide can induce transient orthostatic hypotension, typically occurring within 4 hours of the first few injections. Administer the first dose in a setting where the patient can sit or lie down immediately.

The Consolidation Rule: After completing any finite anabolic course (teriparatide, abaloparatide, or romosozumab), patients must transition immediately to an antiresorptive (bisphosphonates or denosumab) to lock in and build upon the BMD gains. 7D. Rare but Serious Adverse Effects

Osteonecrosis of the Jaw (ONJ)

Incidence: Oral Bisphosphonates: 0.2–10 per 10,000 pt-yrs | Denosumab: ~5.2 per 10,000 pt-yrs

Risk Factors: Active dental pathology, invasive dental procedures (extractions, implants), poor dental hygiene, concurrent chemotherapy or glucocorticoids.

Prevention/Monitoring: Perform dental exams prior to starting therapy; complete major invasive dental work before initiating treatment; maintain routine dental hygiene.

Atypical Femoral Fracture (AFF)

Incidence: 1.78 per 100,000 patient-years (increases with bisphosphonate duration >5 years)

Risk Factors: Duration of therapy >5 years, Asian race/ethnicity, concurrent glucocorticoids, significant femoral bowing.

Prevention/Monitoring: Ask about new thigh or groin pain at every clinical visit. If present, obtain bilateral femur radiographs or MRI. Risk of AFF falls by 50% within 1 year of drug discontinuation.

7E. Glucocorticoid-Induced Osteoporosis (GIOP)

ACR 2022 guidelines recommend clinical fracture assessment for all adults taking ≥2.5 mg prednisone daily for >3 months.

Patient Risk Level Definition (Adults ≥40) Recommended Pharmacotherapy (ACR 2022)

FRAX Adjustments for Prednisone >7.5 mg/day: Increase the calculated 10-year major osteoporotic fracture (MOF) risk by 15% and the hip fracture risk by 20% in the FRAX output.
FDA Approvals in GIOP: Only alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid, teriparatide, and denosumab are FDA-approved for GIOP. Abaloparatide and romosozumab are NOT approved for GIOP.
